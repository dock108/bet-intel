Bet Intel – Phase 2 Product Requirements Document (PRD)

Overview

Bet Intel is a peer-to-peer (P2P) betting analytics platform that helps users identify positive expected value (+EV) betting opportunities across traditional sportsbooks and emerging P2P exchanges. In Phase 1, the core backend for odds aggregation and basic value detection was built. Phase 2 focuses on redesigning the user experience and delivering advanced analytical features that set Bet Intel apart. The goal is to present P2P betting as the “eBay for sports bets” – users bet against each other, not a house ￼ – and to emphasize math transparency as a key differentiator. By clearly explaining how odds are calculated and bets are evaluated, Bet Intel aims to educate newer bettors while providing experienced bettors the tools to exploit market inefficiencies.

This Phase 2 PRD outlines the objectives, new features, UI changes, backend enhancements, and content requirements needed to achieve a more intuitive, informative, and trustworthy platform. It covers a revamped multi-page interface (Dashboard, Search/AI, Education, Disclaimers), the integration of advanced analytics (Bayesian models and Monte Carlo simulations) to rank betting “edges,” a premium AI Recommendation Bot for personalized betting suggestions, and robust educational content to ensure users understand P2P betting and the calculations behind our recommendations. The end result should cater to both novices (guiding them through P2P betting basics) and seasoned bettors (surfacing +EV opportunities with full transparency), all while underlining that Bet Intel provides information only – actual wagering carries risk and success is measured by beating the closing line odds over time ￼.

Objectives
	•	Enhance User Experience: Reorganize the current single-page app into a clear, multi-page interface. Each page will have a distinct purpose (overview of opportunities, search & AI query, educational content, disclaimers/methodology) to reduce confusion and improve navigation. The design will be modern, responsive, and mobile-friendly, making key information easy to scan and understand.
	•	Highlight +EV Opportunities: Surface the best betting opportunities (including player props and alternate markets) using advanced analytics. The Dashboard should automatically showcase the top edges sorted by highest Expected Value (EV) within the next 24 hours. Users can quickly see where potential profit lies, expressed in dollar EV and percent edge, and identify “hot” opportunities at a glance.
	•	Advanced Analytics Completion: Finalize and integrate the Bayesian probability modeling and Monte Carlo simulation features from Phase 1. These will refine how odds discrepancies are evaluated – e.g. using a sharp sportsbook as a Bayesian “prior” and updating with new data – and run simulations to estimate win probabilities more robustly. The objective is to provide more accurate fair odds and confidence intervals for each bet, improving the ranking of edges.
	•	Data Capture for Continuous Improvement: Begin systematically storing closing line odds and game results for all tracked events. This will enable future historical modeling, allow the team (and users) to verify if identified edges did beat the closing line (a key indicator of long-term betting success ￼), and feed machine learning models down the road. Phase 2 aims to lay this groundwork for a data-driven feedback loop on bet predictions.
	•	AI-Powered Betting Assistant: Introduce a premium “AI Recommendation Bot” feature to add a personalized, interactive dimension. Objectives for the bot include understanding natural language queries about bets, generating recommended bets with reasoning, and pointing users to profitable odds. This should increase user engagement and provide extra value (behind a paywall) for serious bettors seeking quick advice.
	•	Education & Transparency: Make Bet Intel an educational platform as much as a betting tool. All calculations (from no-vig fair odds to P2P fee adjustments and EV buffers) will be exposed to users who wish to learn the math. The product will include an Education page that breaks down how P2P betting works and how to use Bet Intel, as well as interactive explanations (tooltips, “learn more” pop-ups, and visualizations) within the UI. By being transparent about the methodology and models, we build user trust and help users make informed decisions.
	•	Responsible Messaging: Clearly communicate that Bet Intel is an analytics service for informational purposes only – it does not place bets or guarantee outcomes. Prominent disclaimers will remind users that sports betting remains risky and that Bet Intel’s goal is to assist in finding value (edges), not to eliminate risk. Emphasize that even +EV bets can lose in the short run; the edge is about long-term probability, and success is measured by beating the market’s closing odds (closing line value) ￼. This manages user expectations and maintains compliance with relevant regulations.

Key Features (Phase 2)
	•	Redesigned Multi-Page Interface: A clean separation of concerns into distinct pages:
	•	Dashboard – shows top EV opportunities across all sports and markets for the next 24 hours.
	•	Search & AI Recommendations – allows users to search for specific teams, players, or ask the AI bot for suggestions.
	•	Education – provides tutorials and explanations about P2P betting and Bet Intel’s calculations.
	•	Disclaimers & Methodology – a static page detailing the platform’s purpose, risk disclaimers, and the mathematical approach used.
	•	Dashboard EV Highlights: A new dashboard with a “Top EV Opportunities” section that displays the highest-value bets available. Each opportunity is presented as a card with key details: sport/league, event matchup, bet description (e.g. “Lakers Win +150”), the recommended odds to list on a P2P exchange, and the calculated EV (in dollar terms or %). Opportunities are tagged with indicators like “🔥 HOT” or “✅ SAFE” based on the EV magnitude or confidence level, to draw attention to significant edges. A summary bar atop the dashboard provides quick stats (e.g. total number of opportunities found, the best EV currently available, time until the next game starts, and number of active bookmakers/exchanges being aggregated).
	•	Advanced EV Calculation Engine: Incorporation of refined algorithms to evaluate betting edges:
	•	No-Vig Fair Odds Calculation: For every market, the system removes the sportsbook’s “juice” to derive the true fair odds ￼. This means converting all book odds to implied probabilities, normalizing to 100% total probability (thus revealing each side’s win probability without the house edge). Rationale: No-vig odds represent the “true” odds of an outcome ￼ ￼, which is the cornerstone for calculating expected value – it tells us what odds would be fair for each side of a bet ￼.
	•	Bayesian Model Adjustment: The platform will use Bayesian modeling to adjust probabilities when appropriate. For example, if there is additional information or a sharper source (like a betting exchange or expert prediction), the model can treat the no-vig probability from a sharp bookmaker as a prior and update it with new data (e.g., recent team performance or line movements). This yields a more nuanced probability estimate that can account for factors beyond the raw odds ￼ (e.g., incorporating priors allows continuous improvement of odds estimates as more information becomes available). This Bayesian-adjusted probability is used to compute a “weighted EV” for each bet, which may differ from the simple EV based on current market odds.
	•	Monte Carlo Simulation: For complex bets or props, Monte Carlo simulations will be utilized to model the range of possible outcomes. By running thousands of simulated game scenarios (using statistical distributions for player performance, scoring, etc.), the system can estimate the probability of a bet winning with more granularity. For instance, to evaluate an NBA player’s over/under prop, the simulation can model the player’s scoring distribution to see how often the over hits. The outcome frequencies from the simulation produce an implied win probability and expected value for the bet. Monte Carlo adds a layer of robustness, as it accounts for variance and correlations that simple static odds might miss. The result for users is an added confidence measure or variance indication for each opportunity (e.g. showing a probability distribution or the likelihood of various profit outcomes).
	•	Edge Ranking & Filtering: All opportunities (across dozens of sportsbooks and P2P exchanges integrated) will be evaluated using the above methods and assigned an EV metric. The system will rank them from highest to lowest EV. On the dashboard and search results, users can filter or sort by EV, time, sport, etc. By default, only positive EV opportunities above a certain threshold will be shown – e.g., requiring at least +2% EV for main markets and +3% for more volatile alternate markets (an “EV buffer” to filter out negligible edges or possible errors). This threshold acts as a margin of safety, ensuring that small edges (which might fall within normal odds fluctuation) are ignored unless they cross a confidence threshold. This approach is to make sure users see only meaningful discrepancies; it acknowledges that frequently betting very tiny edges can increase variance without much benefit, whereas focusing on solid edges (e.g. 3% or greater) is more likely to yield profit ￼. The threshold can be user-adjustable in settings, but defaults will be those mentioned 2–3% for prudence.
	•	AI Recommendation Bot (Premium Feature): A conversational AI assistant that provides on-demand betting insights:
	•	Natural Language Query: Users can type (or speak, via a microphone icon) questions or requests like “Chris Paul over 4.5 assists tonight” or “Any value bets on NFL Sunday?”. The AI will parse the query to identify relevant entities: player/team names, specific stats or markets (e.g. player assists prop), time frames, etc.
	•	Suggested Bets & Odds: The bot responds with one or more suggested bets that align with the query, complete with recommended odds to offer on a P2P exchange. For example, it might answer: “Consider listing Chris Paul Over 4.5 Assists at +120 odds. This line has +EV because most sportsbooks price Over 4.5 at -110 (implying ~52% win probability), but our models give Chris Paul a 55% chance to get 5+ assists. By offering +120, you’d be getting better-than-fair odds on a bet that should hit more often than the price suggests.” The response will be rich in detail, including:
	•	Why it’s +EV: e.g., “Books imply 50% win probability (after vig removal) for this prop, but our Bayesian simulation puts it at ~55%, so the expected value is positive” ￼.
	•	Odds needed for +EV: If the current market odds aren’t favorable, the bot will tell the user what odds would make it a value bet. For instance: “At the moment, the best available odds for Over 4.5 is +105. That’s slightly below fair value – you’d need +110 or better to have at least a 2% edge. If you list at +115 on a P2P exchange and get matched, that would secure roughly a 4% edge.” This gives actionable guidance (either take no action, or what price to seek).
	•	Where it could get filled: Based on market data, the AI can suggest which P2P platform(s) might accept or have liquidity for that bet. For example: “This prop is popular on exchanges like ProphetX and BetOpenly; listing at +120 on those platforms has a good chance of being matched given recent activity in NBA props.” If a certain exchange specializes in the sport/market in question, the bot will mention it. (The list of supported P2P platforms in Phase 2 includes Novig, ProphetX, BetOpenly, etc., per our integrated data feeds.)
	•	Underlying Lines & Model Breakdown: The AI will also summarize relevant data that led to the suggestion, effectively exposing the research behind the recommendation. This could include: “Consensus sportsbook odds: Over 4.5 (-110) vs Under 4.5 (-110) → no-vig win probability ~50%. Model adjustments: accounting for recent performance and opponent’s defense, true Over 4.5 win probability ~55%. Recommended odds (to achieve +EV ≥ 2% after 2% exchange fee): +115 or higher.” All this information will be presented conversationally, but clearly, to justify the advice.
	•	User Experience: The AI bot will be accessible on the “Search & AI” page, possibly as a chat widget or an expansion of the search bar. Paid users can enter queries and receive a detailed answer. The interface will allow follow-up questions, enabling a dialogue (e.g., user: “Why +120 odds?” AI: “At +120, your implied win probability is ~45%; since we estimate the true win probability is 55%, the EV is positive”). This interactive Q&A adds a high-value service for power users. It should feel like having a betting co-pilot that quickly crunches numbers and scans markets on the user’s behalf ￼.
	•	Limitations & Disclaimers: The bot will include a reminder in its responses or UI that it provides recommendations based on data and no outcome is guaranteed ￼. It should encourage responsible betting (e.g., “always consider the risk, no bet is a sure thing”). Technically, it will rely on the up-to-date odds database, the EV engine, and possibly a fine-tuned language model for explanation. Phase 2 scope covers designing the user interaction and defining the output content; the underlying AI model development may use external APIs or existing frameworks (with necessary data fed into the prompts).
	•	Education & Transparency Features: Catering to the dual audience of beginners and advanced bettors, Phase 2 will deliver extensive educational content:
	•	Education Page: A new dedicated page titled “Education” (or “Learn” section) will guide users through:
	•	P2P Betting 101: An explanation of what peer-to-peer betting is and how it differs from traditional sportsbooks. For example, describing that “P2P betting is like eBay for sports bets – instead of the house setting odds, users propose bets to each other, and the platform just takes a small fee” ￼ ￼. Key points: no bookmaker margin (vig) built into odds, typically a ~2% commission from winners ￼, and the ability for users to be the oddsmaker by setting their own odds. This section will likely include a simple step-by-step illustration of how a bet is listed and matched on a P2P exchange (e.g., “You post a bet at X odds → another user accepts → winner gets paid minus fee”).
	•	Understanding Odds & EV: Beginner-friendly tutorials on how odds translate to probabilities and what expected value means. We will define Expected Value as the long-term average outcome of a bet if it could be repeated ￼, and make it clear why +EV bets are desirable (they indicate potential profit over the long run). Simple examples will be provided (e.g., a coin flip at even money has 0 EV, but if you can get better than even money on a fair coin, that’s +EV). We’ll also connect this to Closing Line Value (CLV) – explaining that one way to gauge if a bet was good is to see if you beat the closing odds of the sharp market ￼. It will be noted that consistently “beating the closing line” is correlated with long-term betting profitability ￼, which is exactly what Bet Intel’s recommendations aim to help users do.
	•	How Bet Intel Calculates Recommended Odds: A breakdown (in plain language) of the methodology used by our system (potentially mirroring the “7-Step Methodology” mentioned internally). For example, the page will outline:
	1.	Sharp Baseline – We identify a sharp reference point for each market (often a low-vig sportsbook or exchange considered very efficient).
	2.	No-Vig Fair Odds – Remove the sportsbook’s cut from the odds to get true win probabilities ￼ ￼. This gives the fair odds for each outcome if no fees existed.
	3.	Best Available Line Comparison – We look at all available odds from bookmakers and exchanges to see the best (most favorable) price for each side of a bet. The sharp baseline fair probability is then compared to these available odds to spot initial discrepancies.
	4.	P2P Fee Adjustment – Since P2P exchanges charge a commission (~2% typically ￼), we adjust the fair odds slightly. Essentially, a bet must have a bit more edge to remain +EV after paying the commission. (For example, if fair win probability is 50%, fair odds 2.00 in decimal (+100 American); to profit on a P2P exchange with 2% fee, you might need odds that equate to, say, a 48% or less implied probability, to cover fees.)
	5.	EV Buffer – We impose a small buffer (e.g. require +2% edge for standard bets, +3% for less certain props) before flagging something as a “recommended” value bet. This buffer accounts for model uncertainty and market noise, ensuring we only list bets with a clear edge. (The Education page will explain this concept without too much jargon – perhaps as “We don’t chase pennies: a bet needs to show a significant edge before we recommend it, to protect you from borderline cases.”)
	6.	Identify the Opportunity – If after all adjustments, the odds you can get (or set) on the exchange are still better than the fair odds (plus buffers), then we have a positive EV opportunity. We calculate how much EV (in percentage or dollars for a given stake) that translates to. For instance, “This bet has a +$18 expected profit per $100 staked, based on the probabilities.”
	7.	Cross-Market & Bookmaker Checks – (If applicable) We also highlight if the edge comes from a mismatch between books (e.g., one book is slow to adjust odds) or between books and exchanges. This educates users on why the opportunity exists – maybe an injury news caused divergent odds, etc. The system might label whether it’s a “Book-to-Book” arbitrage situation or a true value vs fair price.
All these steps will be described in an accessible way on the Education page, potentially with small visual aids (like charts showing implied probabilities with and without vig, or a diagram of how an exchange’s commission works). The aim is to let interested users verify and trust the process, and to teach beginners how odds and edges are computed.
	•	How to List a Bet on a P2P Exchange: Since Bet Intel itself is not a betting platform but directs users to take advantage of odds on other exchanges, the Education page will include a tutorial on actually executing the advice. e.g., “If Bet Intel suggests ‘Team A at +150’, you can go to a P2P platform (say ProphetX), find the game and outcome, and enter +150 as your offer. If another user likes the other side, they’ll match your bet.” We’ll cover basics like setting odds, partial match scenarios, and perhaps link to resources of specific exchange platforms if users need step-by-step help. This ensures that users can act on the recommendations even if they are new to P2P betting.
	•	Inline Tooltips and Contextual Help: Beyond the static Education page, the Phase 2 UI will integrate context-sensitive educational tidbits. Examples:
	•	Hovering over a term like “EV” will show a tooltip: “Expected Value (EV): the long-term expected profit of a bet. A +$10 EV means an average profit of $10 per bet if the same conditions occurred many times ￼.”
	•	A small “ℹ️” icon next to “No-Vig” or “Standard vs No-Vig vs Weighted” could explain: “Standard EV uses the raw book odds, No-Vig EV uses fair odds with no house cut (giving a more accurate baseline ￼), Weighted EV incorporates our Bayesian model adjustments for a refined estimate.” This way even on the Dashboard, curious users can learn what these figures mean.
	•	The “🔥 HOT” or other labels can have explainer text: e.g. “HOT: This opportunity has very high EV (top 10%) compared to all others right now – it might be due to a notable odds discrepancy. Still not guaranteed to win, but statistically very favorable.”
	•	We will also consider a “Learn More” expandable section on the Dashboard cards or Search results. For each bet, users could click to see the detailed breakdown (similar to what the AI bot would output): showing the odds from different sources, the implied probabilities, and how the EV was calculated step by step. This is essentially exposing the model’s reasoning for those who want it. For example, a user clicking “View Details” on a matchup might see a mini-report: “Pinnacle odds imply 40% win chance, DraftKings 42%, Bet Intel fair win probability 45% after simulation, your offered odds imply 38% – giving you a 7% edge. Calculations include removal of 4.5% vig from Pinnacle’s odds ￼ and a 2% exchange fee adjustment.” Presenting these numbers visually (perhaps with a bar chart of probabilities) can make it digestible.
	•	Visualization of Model Outputs: For power users, Bet Intel will offer optional visual analytics. For instance:
	•	Distribution Graphs: If Monte Carlo simulation is run for a given bet (say total points in a game), the user could toggle a graph that shows the distribution of simulated outcomes (e.g., a bell curve of total points, highlighting the percentage of sims where the total goes over a certain line). For example, a small chart could show that in the last 100 recommended bets with ~5% EV, 55 of them won (which would be a positive outcome overall). This transparency lets users confirm that, for example, Bet Intel’s suggestion of +150 is indeed better than any sportsbook’s offer on that side. Essentially, “we recommend you offer +150 on the exchange because the best you could otherwise get is +140 at DraftKings – so you’re getting a better deal than any sportsbook if someone takes it.” Seeing traditional sportsbook odds side-by-side with exchange potential odds can be very persuasive, and we intend to show these comparisons.

Pages Breakdown (UI Restructuring)

Phase 2 introduces four primary pages in the application. Each page’s purpose, content, and features are described below:

Dashboard: This will be the home screen and visual centerpiece of Bet Intel’s UI. It provides a quick overview of the best betting opportunities identified by the system in the next 24 hours (focusing on upcoming games). Key elements of the Dashboard include:
	•	Top Stats Bar: At the top, a summary panel displays high-level stats: e.g. “95 Opportunities” (total number of +EV opportunities currently found), “+$24.50 Best EV” (the highest expected profit on a single bet, perhaps for a $100 stake, available right now), “45m Next Game” (countdown to the next game’s start time among those we’re tracking), and “6 Active Books” (number of sportsbooks/exchanges currently being scanned). This gives users situational awareness as soon as they log in.
	•	Filters & Controls: Users can filter the view by sport or league (e.g., NBA, NFL, MLB, etc.), by market type (spread, moneyline, over/under, props), or by time (e.g., “Today Only” to see games within 24h). There is also a sort control (default “Best EV” descending). For example, a user could toggle to only show NBA opportunities or only those above a certain EV dollar value. These filters were partially present in Phase 1 and will be refined for clarity in Phase 2.
	•	Top EV Opportunities List: The main section shows a list or grid of the highest EV opportunities. Each Opportunity Card is a module containing:
	•	Event and Context: The matchup or event name (e.g., “Lakers @ Warriors” with date/time), sport/league tag (e.g., NBA icon), and maybe an indicator if it’s a main market or a prop. If the bet is a player prop or a specific market, that will be noted (e.g., “LeBron Points O/U” or “First Half Spread”).
	•	Recommended Bet Details: Within the card, it will say something like “Lakers Win @ +150” (meaning the recommendation is to take Lakers moneyline at +150 odds on a P2P exchange). Next to it, the Expected Value is shown, e.g. “+$24.50 EV”, which likely means if you wager $100, you expect to make $24.50 on average. We use a dollar format for EV to be tangible, but we might also include a percentage (like +24.5% EV in this case) or allow a toggle between the two. The card might highlight this EV in green or with a flame 🔥 icon if it’s among the top ones. In the screenshot, labels like “HOT” (flame icon) or “GOOD” or “SAFE” appear – these can be tied to thresholds of EV or perhaps qualitative model confidence. For instance, “HOT” might mean EV above a certain high threshold (e.g. > $20 or > 10%), “SAFE” might indicate a moderate edge that is deemed low-risk (maybe when the favored outcome has an edge), etc. These labels provide quick cues to users skimming the list.
	•	Source of Edge: We list which sportsbook or source is used as the basis. In the example, “DraftKings” is shown on the Lakers @ Warriors card. This likely indicates that DraftKings’ odds on the opposing side or some line at DraftKings is what gives this opportunity (perhaps DraftKings has Warriors -170, implying Lakers fair price should be +170 no-vig, but you can get +150 on exchange, making it profitable). In other cases, it might say multiple books or “Consensus” if the edge is versus the market consensus. We will clarify this label, possibly calling it “Best Reference” or “Source Odds”.
	•	EV Breakdown: For transparency, each card can show a breakdown of the EV calculation. In the screenshot, under the Lakers @ Warriors card, it lists Standard, No-Vig, and Weighted with corresponding values (+$18.20, +$21.40, +$24.50). This indicates:
	•	Standard EV: Using raw odds (with vig) as the basis, the EV is $18.20.
	•	No-Vig EV: Using fair odds without vig, EV is $21.40 (higher, because removing vig makes the bet relatively more favorable).
	•	Weighted EV: Using the Bayesian/advanced model probability, EV is $24.50 (even higher, suggesting our model is more bullish on this bet than the market is).
We will include these if space allows, or perhaps show them on hover/tap. This breakdown is crucial for advanced users to see how different assumptions affect EV. It also illustrates the impact of our advanced features (the difference between standard and weighted EV is the “edge” our modeling adds).
	•	Actions: Each card will have a “View Details” button, which can expand more info (as discussed in Education/Transparency – showing full odds comparison, etc.). There might also be a favorite/alert icon (heart or bell). If a user clicks a heart, we could save that opportunity to a personal list or set up an alert if that opportunity is about to disappear (this may tie into user accounts/Phase 3, but the UI can be placed now). “View Details” could either open a modal or navigate to a detailed page per event. In Phase 2, a modal overlay might be simpler for now.
	•	Upcoming Events / Calendar: The dashboard might also include a secondary section like “Upcoming Events” or a calendar view (as hinted by a “View Calendar” link in the screenshot). This could list games coming up later today or tomorrow, along with how many EV opportunities each has (e.g., “7:30 PM ET Lakers @ Warriors – 3 EVs (Best: +$24.5)”). This is more of a navigational aid: a user might click on a specific game to see all opportunities for that game (if they’re only interested in certain teams). It also shows that Bet Intel covers multiple events, not just singular picks, giving a sense of coverage breadth.
	•	Responsiveness: The dashboard will be designed to work on desktop and mobile. On mobile, the top stats might turn into a swipeable carousel or just 2-tiles per row, and the opportunity cards likely stack vertically.

Overall, the Dashboard is about quickly informing the user “What should I bet on today?” by highlighting the best few wagers with context and letting them drill down for details. It balances brevity (headline EV and odds) with depth (drilldowns into methodology if wanted).

Search & AI Recommendations: This page serves two closely related functions: a traditional search for odds/value opportunities, and an entry point to interact with the AI recommendation bot. The design integrates these so that a user can either type a keyword (team, player, league) to filter existing opportunities or ask a question to trigger the AI’s analysis. Key components:
	•	Search Bar with Query Input: At the top, a prominent search field allows text input of teams, events, or even natural-language queries. The placeholder might say “Search teams, events, sports… or ask for betting ideas”. A microphone icon is present for voice input (especially useful on mobile). The user can press it to speak a query (which the app will transcribe). This is particularly convenient if someone wants to quickly ask, “What’s the best bet for the Yankees game?” without typing.
	•	Search Filters: Just below the search bar, there are quick filter buttons for sports (NBA, NFL, MLB, NHL, etc.), and other toggles like “All Books”, “EV > $5”, “Today Only”. The bot might respond educating that P2P exchanges don’t support parlays or that parlays usually diminish EV. So handling such queries gracefully (maybe out of scope for now, but we should at least not break).
	•	“What’s the expected value of betting on the Chiefs?” – The bot can interpret that as asking if the Chiefs (in their upcoming game) have +EV at current odds.
	•	“Should I list or take this bet?” – If someone is specifically unsure about lay vs back, the bot can clarify (e.g., “Listing the favorite = you act as the bookmaker on the favorite, which is equivalent to taking the underdog. If you want to back the favorite, you’d take someone else’s offer on the exchange.” This is more educational, but useful).
	•	These scenarios help fine-tune the bot’s features.

Out of Scope (for Phase 2, potential future enhancements):
	•	Live Betting Advice: The bot will not handle live in-game betting queries in Phase 2. All responses are about pre-game markets (for games that haven’t started). Live odds move too fast and require real-time analysis that we are not ready to support. So if asked about something like “2nd quarter live odds”, the bot will likely respond that live betting support isn’t available yet.
	•	Arbitrage or Sure-bet detection: While our system might incidentally spot arbitrages (where you can guarantee profit by betting both sides at different books), the bot will not specifically guide users through multi-bet arbitrage strategies in this phase. It focuses on single-bet value. (We may add an arb mode in the future, but it’s a different use case.)
	•	Bet Execution: The bot will not place bets for the user or directly interface with exchanges to create bets. It only provides the info and suggestions. (Perhaps in a much later phase, integration for one-click bet listing could be explored, but not now.)
	•	General Sports Q&A: The bot is not for general sports trivia or news (e.g., “Who won last night?” or “What is the Patriots’ record?”). It may know some of these if we feed data, but it’s not our focus. We’ll tune it to pivot back to betting context (“I’m here to help with betting questions, for scores and records you might check ESPN…”).
	•	Customization of Risk Profile: Phase 2 bot gives standard recommendations. In future, we might allow the user to say “I want only very safe bets” or “show me high risk high reward”. That’s not in scope now – all users get the same value-focused suggestions (which inherently lean towards underdogs often, which some might consider risky, but that’s just how value works).
	•	Multiple Bets or Portfolio Advice: The bot currently handles queries one at a time. It won’t create a full betting plan or manage bankroll advices like “bet $X” (though we could use Kelly criterion to suggest stake size, we’ll hold off unless we feel confident to present that – perhaps advanced user toggle).
	•	Complex Multi-step Conversations: We have minimal context carry-over. We won’t handle things like “Remember that bet from yesterday…” or cross-session memory. Each session is short-lived.

In summary, Phase 2’s AI bot is scoped to be a helpful assistant for recommending and explaining bets based on our system’s data, within the boundaries of pre-game betting and our data coverage. It draws on similar concepts to existing AI betting assistants, e.g., providing specific odds suggestions and rationales ￼, but unique in that it integrates our P2P-focused EV analysis. The success criterion is that users find it saves them time (they can ask instead of manually searching) and provides clarity (they understand why a bet is suggested). We will monitor its usage and feedback to refine it further in subsequent phases.

Education & Transparency UX Details

Education and transparency are core values for Bet Intel, and their implementation spans both content and design elements. This section consolidates how the UX will foster user learning and trust through transparency:
	•	Optional “Education Mode”: We will consider a mode or toggle (especially for advanced users) that, when activated, makes the interface more data-rich. For instance, turning on “Show Advanced Metrics” in user settings could always display things like the Standard/No-vig/Weighted EV breakdown on cards (instead of requiring hover), show the implied probabilities next to odds, and possibly color-code values by edge size. This way, novice users by default see a clean simple view (“Bet this, EV +$X”), while experts can opt into seeing all the granular numbers on every screen. Phase 2 will implement some of these as prototypes – e.g., a toggle on the Dashboard to reveal more columns or details.
	•	Visualization Tools: Some ideas for visual components to aid understanding:
	•	Odds Heatmaps: For a given game, display a small chart of all sportsbooks’ odds vs exchange odds. Perhaps a bar graph: one bar per bookmaker’s moneyline for Team A and Team B, and a highlighted bar for the “fair odds” and for “recommended odds”. This immediately shows, for example, that all books have Team A between -150 and -170, fair no-vig implies -160, and we suggest listing -155 to get a tiny edge. A user can visually see our number in context. Such a graphic could appear in the detailed view modal.
	•	Probability Distribution Graph: Particularly for totals and props – show a curve or histogram for the distribution of outcomes from our Monte Carlo simulation. Mark the betting line on the graph to illustrate how much area (probability) lies on each side of it. This communicates why one side is favored by our model. It turns abstract stats into a picture that can be easier to digest.
	•	EV Over Time Chart: Possibly track how the EV of a certain bet changes as the odds move (closing in on game time). This could be in a detail view, indicating line movement. E.g., “This was +3% EV earlier today and is +5% now, meaning the odds have shifted further in our favor (or our probability estimate changed).” It teaches users that timing and line movement matter. This might leverage our odds history data.
	•	We will use visual cues like color coding to reinforce concepts: green for positive EV, red for negative EV (or opportunities that turned negative), etc. Already in dev screenshots, we see red percentages for negative EV and green for positive. We’ll ensure consistency: any positive edge or favorable condition is in a positive color, negative in a caution color.
	•	Interactive Demos/Tutorials: On first login or access, we might show a guided tour: highlighting “This is the Dashboard where we list top bets” – “Here is the EV value, which is what you could gain on average ￼” – “Click Education to learn more” – etc. This onboarding can dramatically improve understanding for new users. Phase 2 can include a simple version (maybe a modal with pointers or even just a welcome message pointing to the Education section).
	•	Community & Support Integration: Not exactly education, but transparency also means being responsive to user questions. We might integrate a link to a community forum or a support chat where users can ask “I don’t get why this EV is calculated as such” and get an answer. While building a community is beyond this PRD’s scope, having a “Feedback” or “Ask a question” button on the Education page can be helpful. It shows we’re open about our methods and ready to explain if something isn’t clear.
	•	Ensuring Simplicity Amidst Complexity: One UX challenge is to not overwhelm less-experienced users with too many numbers. The plan is to layer information. For example, the default card shows just the key recommendation and EV. Only if the user engages (clicks “details” or toggles advanced) do they see the intricacies like probabilities from different sources. All advanced content will be present but tucked away by default behind collapsible sections, modals, or toggles. This caters to both audiences effectively.
	•	Terminology Consistency: We will standardize terms across the app and explain them. E.g., if we use “Edge” and “EV” interchangeably, that might confuse some – we might choose one (likely EV) and stick to it, but explain “edge (expected value)” somewhere. The glossary of terms might be part of the Education page or a tooltip system. Terms like “vig” or “juice” we should always accompany with explanation until the user seems to get it. Perhaps the first time they see “no-vig”, it’s underlined and clicking it shows definition. These little touches make the platform more accessible.
	•	Transparency of Limitations: In our educational content or maybe in a blog, we might also be transparent about what our models don’t do yet. For example, “Our current model does not account for very advanced player synergy or weather effects explicitly, it primarily uses market odds and basic stats. We are continually improving this.” This honesty can earn user trust, showing we’re not overclaiming infallibility.
	•	User Education Metrics: We could track engagement with education features (like how many opened the methodology details, how many toggled advanced view) to gauge interest and adjust focus. But that’s more an analytics concern.
	•	Regulatory Transparency: In disclaimers and possibly in UI, we’ll identify ourselves (the company) and any relevant licensing or compliance info (if needed, e.g., “Bet Intel is not a sportsbook, we do not handle wagers. Always follow your local laws.”). Being upfront can prevent misunderstandings (someone might think we’re an exchange if not paying attention; we need them to know we’re a tool, not a betting provider).

By implementing the above, Bet Intel’s UX will not only deliver recommendations but also foster understanding. The intended outcome is that users actually become more knowledgeable bettors through using our platform – they can learn why a bet is good or bad and eventually even identify opportunities on their own. This builds goodwill and stickiness (they’ll keep using the platform because it’s enriching their skillset, not just feeding picks).

In Phase 2, we’ll establish these educational pillars, and in future phases, we can expand them (maybe offering courses, webinars, or a community around beating the market, etc.). For now, a user should conclude after using Phase 2 product: “I trust these recommendations because I can see how they’re derived, and I actually learned a thing or two about betting value and P2P betting that I didn’t know before.”

Legal Disclaimers and Compliance Considerations

Ensuring legal and ethical compliance is essential for Bet Intel, especially given the sensitive nature of sports betting. Below is a summary of the disclaimers and measures we will implement to address legal requirements and manage liability:
	•	Prominent Disclaimer Display: Upon a user’s first visit or sign-up, present a disclaimer notice that they must acknowledge. This could be a check box or click-through that states they understand Bet Intel provides information only and not betting services. We will also include a short reminder on relevant pages (e.g., at the bottom of the Dashboard or AI response, a line like “All betting is risky – informational use only”). We won’t nag returning users excessively, but the information will always be one click away (via the dedicated page).
	•	No Betting Transactions on Platform: Clarify within the UI (perhaps in a tooltip on the Dashboard “How to use Bet Intel” onboarding) that to actually place a bet, the user must go to a licensed sportsbook or exchange. Phrases like “Use Bet Intel to find value, then place your bets at your preferred sportsbook or P2P exchange” make it clear we are separate from the actual wagering process. This distinction helps us avoid regulatory classification as a betting operator.
	•	Age Gate: During account creation (or if we allow usage without account, perhaps when accessing certain features), we should ensure the user confirms they are of legal age (e.g., “By proceeding, I affirm I am over 18/21 and legally permitted to gamble in my jurisdiction.”). This may be in the Terms of Service acceptance.
	•	Responsible Gambling Messaging: Though Bet Intel is not directly taking bets, we are part of the gambling ecosystem. It’s good practice (and in some places required) to encourage responsible gambling. We might include a small note or link, such as “Gambling Problem? Call 1-800-GAMBLER” in the footer or disclaimers section. This shows we are conscientious of user well-being.
	•	Privacy Compliance: (Not explicitly asked, but worth noting) – If we have an AI bot and tracking, ensure our privacy policy covers how user data and query content are used. Also if using third-party APIs (like OpenAI), we should not send personally identifiable info there.
	•	Geolocation Advisory: Since we won’t restrict who can view odds (as it’s just info), we should at least state “It is the user’s responsibility to comply with local laws regarding sports betting. Bet Intel’s content is void where prohibited.” This covers us if someone in a banned state tries to use our advice to gamble illegally.
	•	Intellectual Property: Possibly mention that odds data is sourced from third parties and for personal use, etc., but that’s more terms of use.
	•	Methodology as Non-Advice: We might explicitly say that any strategy or method described (like beating CLV) is not a guarantee and is just a theory supported by historical data ￼. And that users shouldn’t assume it will always work – essentially, it’s educational content, not an assured method to win money.
	•	Beta/Product Status: If Phase 2 is effectively still a beta, we might add a note: “Beta version – data may not always be accurate or updated, use at your own risk.” But that might undermine trust; if we include it, we do so carefully or perhaps skip if we’re confident in quality.
	•	Third-Party Links: If we link out to exchanges or sportsbooks (e.g., an “offer” button might open Betfair, etc.), we will mention we are not endorsing or affiliated (unless we have affiliate deals, in which case disclosures might be needed per regulations). The disclaimer page can list any affiliate relationships transparently.
	•	Content Moderation for AI: Ensure the AI bot does not stray into problematic territory. For example, user might ask “How do I guarantee a win?” – the bot should not give irresponsible advice. It should stick to facts and probabilities, reinforcing that no bet is guaranteed ￼. This is both an ethical and a legal consideration (we don’t want to be seen as encouraging reckless betting).
	•	Logging and Auditing: Internally, log what recommendations we give – if ever questioned (say a user complains about being misled), we have a record of what we presented. Also, this helps in refining disclaimers if we notice common misconceptions.
	•	Terms and Conditions Document: In addition to the brief disclaimers, a full Terms of Service will be prepared (not necessarily part of PRD text, but as a deliverable) covering all legal bases (liability limits, governing law, etc.). The PRD doesn’t detail this, but it’s understood as part of launching such a product.

In summary, Bet Intel will operate with a safety-first approach when it comes to legal and user protection. The messaging “informational only, not risk-free, designed to beat CLV in the long run” will be front and center. By educating users on what that means (i.e., beating CLV consistently is a positive sign but not a guarantee of immediate profits ￼), we align expectations and stay on the right side of gambling advice regulations.

Implementing these disclaimers and ethical guidelines not only shields us legally but also bolsters our credibility as a transparent, user-first platform.
